Knitspionage

Salzburg, 1943. You are a courier travelling by rail, your knitting basket is your cover. Everything you see from this window must reach the resistance — encoded in wool, row by row.

During both World Wars, knitters really were suspected of — and in some cases used for — encoding messages in their stitch patterns. Irregularities invisible to a casual glance could carry real intelligence. This game lets you try the craft yourself.

Orders

Your Instructions

"Five sightings. Encode each in your stitches as you ride. Keep your pattern clean — too many mistakes, and the wrong eyes may notice. Deliver the finished piece in your destination. Godspeed."

Each time you look out the window, you'll see something worth reporting. Translate it to Morse, then knit it: knit = dot · purl = dash. The cheat sheet stays with you throughout.

Outcome

Mission Complete

Delivered Safely

You arrive in your destination to hand over the scarf. Five sightings, now hidden in wool, are on their way to those who need them.

The real history: Knitters in WWI and WWII Belgium reportedly tracked train movements from windows overlooking railway lines, recording troop counts and timings in patterns for the resistance.

Final suspicion level: —

Mission Failed

Your Cover Is Blown

The conductor's eyes linger too long on your needles. At the next stop, two officers are waiting on the platform. Your basket is searched.

Real couriers relied on absolute consistency — an irregular stitch in an otherwise plain pattern was exactly the kind of detail a trained eye could catch. Precision was the whole craft.
